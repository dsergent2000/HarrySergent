“Those Early Days” 1 Harry always planned to complete his novel on Early Aviation, but sadly time (and molecular degeneration) caught up with him. When completed, it was to consist of two broad sections. The first part was to be about Smithy and his achievements. This was not only to include Harry's experiences meeting him and working for him, but also to list Smithy’s achievements, and to correct any misinformation that had been published about him. The second section was going to focus on Harry’s experiences after Smithy's passing. Here, Harry was going to focus on both the work & cultural experiences he witnessed, in many exotic countries around the world. Here are sections Harry’s memoirs which he wrote himself, and were to be the start of “Those Were The Days”. In August and September 1992 while on a tour of the U. K. and Europe I met another Tourist, an American by the name of Bill. While on such tours it was first name only. During a discussion with Bill he told me that he had worked in early Aviation a few years after the First World War and prior to that he had worked as a mechanic on some early cars, or ‘Autos’ as he described them. This was of course very interesting to me because I also worked in early aviation and before that I served part of my early apprenticeship on Model T and Model A Ford cars, and later on a range of Chrysler cars. I started work in 1928, which was a little later than when Bill started. However, we both had some interesting things to talk about. On one occasion I mentioned something I have often said to others to emphasise how much progress has been made in aviation in the lifetime of those of my age. I was talking about the time it took Imperial Airways to fly from London to Johannesburg in South Africa in 11 days with 10 night stops. To continue to Cape Town it took another day making a total time of 12 days with 11 night stops. When I started work with Imperial Airways (a predecessor of British Airways) in 1936, the timetable (which I still have) was the same as 1932 with two or three minor changes to night stops, but the total time to Johannesburg and Cape Town was the same. Listening to the discussion between Bill and I, was another person on the tour and she said, "that's ridiculous, why did it take so long"? My first reaction left me stunned for a few seconds and I then turned to her and said "Maxine, were your children able to walk and run from the time they were born"? I don't know whether she got the message because she said no more. In between breaks in the two tours, my Brother-in-Law told me he would like to take me to two Aviation Museums, knowing my interest in vintage aircraft. My visit to "The Shuttleworth Collection" at Old Warden Aerodrome and Duxford which claims to be the biggest and best aeroplane museum in Europe, showed the progress made over 54 years with two aircraft on display, which was of particular interest to me. Shuttleworth had a 1915 Avro 504K aircraft, which was of special interest to me because it was an Avro 504K that I had my first flight in an aeroplane, and I will have more to mention about this marvellous old aircraft for it's age, later on. Duxford had a Concorde on show and this sleek aircraft of Anglo/ French design created a lot of interest worldwide when the two maiden flights took place. The French prototype took flight on March 2, 1969 and the British prototype on April 9, 1969. Both aircraft produced a cruising speed of Mach 2 (twice the speed of sound), in November 1970. Also of special interest to me was a flight made by British Airways in March 1985 when a British Airways Concorde broke the London to Cape Town record, a distance of 9,650 km in 8 hours and 8 minutes. It was emphasised at that time that it clipped 3 hours off a Boeing 747 record in 1977 which was considered a great achievement. It took my memory back to when I worked at most of the night stops and refuelling stops on that route. The same company then operating as Imperial Airways had a schedule for London to Cape Town of 12 days with 11 night stops (as already mentioned). The rate of progress since that time has left the majority of people today with no idea of the early days of air transport, but what we have today would not have been possible without the work of the early pioneers, who should not be forgotten. Maxine's comment made me think that I should write a little book about my experiences in those early days, and limit it to that era, because I believe that many people seem to forget, that everything has a beginning. However, an ignorant remark by an American Pilot, and two books published in the 90's besmirched Sir Charles Kingsford Smith's character, and I feel compelled to add my comment about this in my book, because these people should not be allowed to get away with what they said. Kingsford Smith, affectionately known as ‘Smithy’ didn't deserve such treatment after all he did for the development of aviation. There were two letters in the Sydney Morning Herald in 1991 that referred to an American Co-pilot who said, when announcing the impending arrival at Sydney Kingsford Smith Airport that the airport was named after an Aussie aviator who flew across from New Zealand in an old tri-motor. The ignorance of this person saying that, after all that Sir Charles Kingsford Smith had done, requires more to be said about his achievements, because there may be many others who are also completely ignorant of Smithy's pioneering records. What I have to say about Smithy is not for the converted, but for those who seem to know very little about this great aviation pioneer’s achievements. I received a letter from Ian Mackersey dated 21 April 1992, advising me that he had been commissioned by his publisher in London to write a major new biography on the life of Smithy, and he was advised by Arch Steinbeck (who was then the Vice President of the Australian Early Birds of Australia and later President) that I might possibly be able to help him with some reminiscences of Smithy. This led to fourteen letters of correspondence between us. During this time Scotty Allan's book "Scotty Allan, Australia's Flying Scotsman" was published and this resulted in more correspondence as I felt something had to be done to rectify the awful untruths said about Smithy, by Scotty. Following this Ian Mackersey had his book "Smithy, The Life of Sir Charles Kingsford Smith" was published 63 years after Smithy's death. This needs some comment about how he set out very late after Smithy's death to rely on unreliable people such as Scotty Allan, when he had good reason to doubt what Scotty had told him on so many of his opinions about Smithy. In addition, Ian Mackersey knew that Scotty actually fabricated many things that did not have an atom of truth in his book. Scotty is now dead but what I have to say about him with regards to his book will be no more than what I wrote to Dick Smith, with copies to Scotty Allan (when he was alive) and Elizabeth Shearman (Scotty's biographer). All letters were sent to these people by registered mail. Elizabeth Shearman said in the introduction of Scotty's book, "Scotty's wife, Barbara, provided encouragement, good advice and many excellent cups of tea! She also had the good sense to send the manuscript to Dick Smith. Last but certainly not least, Dick Smith should be congratulated for his perspicacity and appreciation of "a great aviation story. Without Dick's enthusiastic support, the book would not have got off the runway." It was for this reason that I wrote to Dick Smith on the 15th July 1993 and I told him that after reading "Scotty Allan, Australia's Flying Scotsman" I was amazed that things said by Scotty were completely fabricated and I made reference to these. I was advised that there were other Early Birds who also wrote to Dick about the disgusting made - up stories Scotty had to say about Smithy with the intention to besmirch him. I received a letter back from the Australian Geographic Pty Ltd, dated 30 July 1993, which said: "Dick has asked me to thank you very much for your letter of 15 July. Dick has asked me to say that he will await an answer from Scotty Allan and Elizabeth Shearman at which point he will be able to resolve any discrepancies and have these corrected and/or add in any other opinions. Thank you once again for writing to Dick”. Yours sincerely, Natalie Ranald, Assistant to Dick Smith. I heard no more from Dick, Scotty or Elizabeth Shearman. However, I have more to say about this further on in my book, which is very important that people should know the truth about Scotty's book, in fairness to Sir Charles Kingsford Smith. Of course nothing can be done to rectify what has been said by Scotty in the books that have been sold. I made enquiries to the publishers long after I wrote to Dick Smith and nothing had been done to withdraw any books that had not been sold at that time. There was another case I have to mention, where a messenger of a person who said what is known as "the oil transfer in mid air" never took place, and in doing so this person also besmirched P.G.Taylor's character. P.G. was a very honourable man who won the George Cross for outstanding bravery for what he did when the oil transfer took place. This now adds four stories to my original intention of what I intended to write about, but all must be told, because neither Smithy or P.G. deserved the outrageous comments that some have said about these two great men. Readers may wonder why someone at 89 had left it so late to write this book, which I had in mind back in 1992. An ironical turn of events with regards to aerodromes upset the retirement plans of my wife and I, at the wrong time in our lives. Our case was a front line story together with our photos in a leading local paper which emphasised the problems residents had with properties under the Australian Noise Exposure Forecast (ANEF), relating to the proposed Airport at Badgery’s Creek outside of Sydney. This had a devastating affect on people who had to sell their property because of age or sickness, and who had purchased their land long before it was decided to build the aerodrome at Badgery’s Creek. This applied to the very best of properties that previously were easy to sell, but due to restrictions placed on properties under the ANEF contour it was a different story, and no compensation applied to many properties, even under the low ANEF readings which had the same restrictions placed on them in some cases. Although this was not something in the original agenda of my book of what I had planned to write about back in 1992, I believe it will not only explain the reason for the delay I had in writing this book, but it will show the good and bad side of the advancement of aviation and the price many have had to pay for what we have today. However, that's a different episode in my life and I will leave that story for later on. Domestic responsibilities and the loss of sight of my right eye due to molecular degeneration with an advanced cataract in my left eye, plus the beginning of molecular degeneration in my right eye, resulted in having to switch my typing to a computer and this slowed me up for a while. But I wish I had made the switch much earlier because of the benefits that a computer can give. Raising the font size from 10 to 22 did the trick as far as assisting my eyesight. MY EARLY INTEREST IN AVIATION Kingsford Smith, Charles Ulm as co-pilot, Jim Warner as navigator and Harry Lyon as wireless operator, made the first successful Pacific flight, between 31st May and 9th June 1928. This was and still is considered the first of the two greatest pioneering flights ever made, considering the time that this took place. In both cases Kingsford Smith was involved. However, it was not until Kingsford Smith, Ulm, Harold Litchfield as navigator, and Tom McWilliams as wireless operator, who made the first successful flight across the Tasman Sea from Richmond Aerodrome outside of Sydney to Wigram Aerodrome in Christchurch NZ, a distance of 1,624 miles (in 14 hours and 25 minutes) that I really became totally interested in aviation. It was through Kingsford Smith and all his aviation achievements that lead me into a career in aviation, like it did to many youth and young men in Australia and New Zealand at that time. In my case, early aviation gave me the opportunity to work in many countries that I would never have been able to do, if I had been a fare-paying passenger. For that reason, apart from having a very great admiration for what Smithy achieved, I owe him a lot for giving me the initial start in aviation, and that gave me the privilege to work in such an interesting period of aviation. I can remember that first successful flight to NZ very well, although I was then only two weeks away from my 14th birthday. The publicity build up before the flight was tremendous, and with this publicity was the speculation about the safety of the Tasman attempt, especially as it was on the 10th of January (the same year that Hood and Moncrieff disappeared after attempting the Tasman crossing). That was a very sad time in NZ because so much was expected of these two pilots and both were Reserve Officers in the Royal NZ Air Force. Hood was a World War 1 pilot with a wooden leg. Both were pilots with considerable experience, and they had a good aircraft for that era, a Ryan, very similar to the Ryan used by Lindbergh's "Spirit of St.Louis" on his Trans-Atlantic flight. They had a radio but neither Hood or Moncrieff were able to operate it, so it was designed to send out signals every 15 minutes that would advise radio stations that all was ok. These signals were received up to an estimated 200 miles from NZ, and two ships actually said that they had spotted the Ryan. About their estimated time of arrival in NZ, a violent storm in the Tasman sea was reported and following an extensive search for the two airman, it had to finally be concluded that they were lost, and their tragedy lingered on in NZ for a very long time. TRANS PACIFIC CROSSING On the 31st May, 1928, Kingsford Smith and his 4-man crew left Oakland, California, to attempt the first trans-Pacific flight to Australia. They took off from Barking Sands on Mana, Kauai (since the runway at Wheeler was not long enough). They headed for Suva, Fiji, 5,077 kilometres away, taking 34 hours 30 minutes. This was the most demanding portion of the journey, as they flew through a massive lightning storm near the equator. The third leg was the shortest, 2,709 kilometres in 20 hours, and crossed the Australian coastline near Ballina, before turning north to fly 170 kilometres to Brisbane, where they landed at 10.50 a.m. on 9 June. The total flight distance was approximately 11,566 kilometres. Kingsford Smith was met by a huge crowd of 26,000 at Eagle Farm Airport, and was welcomed as a hero. Here, I quote Smithy and on the lessons he learnt on their record breaking flight, “We had certainly learned a great deal, and the outstanding object lesson was the paramount importance of the ability to fly blind for long periods in bad weather, such as in rain, cloud and fog, to which I have made previous reference". Smithy continued on, focussing on their Suva landing, “These long distance flights are not accomplished without a succession of trials and anxieties, and I now began to think about our landing ground. We had previously telegraphed to Suva, prior to leaving Oakland, for details of the Albert Park Sports Oval, which was the only spot where we could land. The maximum runway, however, was barely 1200ft, in addition to which there were some awkward telegraph wires and three trees at the north-west corner, which we had asked to be removed.” “We were now in a fairly light condition, but as we circled round Suva I anxiously looked out for the landing ground. After over 30 hours in the air I knew that I was not altogether as bright and sharp-witted as when we took off. The strain of the blind flying and the bombardment of the rainstorms had added to my fatigue, and further more, I had never seen the Albert Park ground before the landing.” “As I came down I suddenly caught sight of a sudden drop of about 12ft, from the roadway to the field, and we were coming along at 65 miles per hour. We touched the ground half way up the field. In front of me was a sharp rise, clothed in trees and thick undergrowth. I had to ground loop the plane and swing to the left as we came to a stop". It cleared a tree by 4 feet. One man amongst the locals was an ex World War 1 pilot who gave a Radio commentary of Smithy's approach into Albert Park, and he could see nothing but a doomsday scenario of the situation as Smithy was in, after such a successful flight, over such a long stretch of the Pacific Ocean. Smithy felt apologetic about the arrangements the locals had made and the inadequate Albert Park. However, there appeared to be nowhere else within Suva, suitable for an aircraft the size of the Southern Cross to take off or land. The next day, the first thing Smithy had to do was to look for a possible place for a take off with the Southern Cross (which needed to be fully loaded with petrol for their last hop to Australia). Local pilots recommended four sites that might of been suitable. While Ulm, Lyon and Warner visited the three sites closest to Suva, Smithy went by the Governor's launch twenty miles from Suva to Naselei Beach. This turned out to be an excellent site, with very firm sand and according to Smithy, the best runway he had seen. The only problem was getting the petrol to the sight and this slowed matters up, and naturally Smithy and Ulm were anxious to get to Australia as soon as possible. After making arrangements for petrol to be sent to Naselei Beach, the next move for Smithy was to fly the Southern Cross out of Albert Park. As it was light with fuel he got it out after a short run of 400 yards, although he had the 12ft rise ahead of him (just another example of Smithy's flying ability). I would like to mention Smithy's words again when he said "I should like to discuss our Pacific flight and to review the lessons which it taught us". I have mentioned some of those difficulties such as the way navigation had to be carried out, and the limits of the Southern Cross to climb above bad weather and the strain on all the crew, who had to carry out precisely what they had to do, over such long and strenuous times. This will allow readers to know the restrictions of what those early airman had to contend with, when Smithy and Ulm made the first Pacific flight (and all the other pioneering flights made by Smithy). FIRST NON STOP FLIGHT ACROSS AUSTRALIA This was the next important step (and record breaking moment) for Smithy. They chose the RAAF base at Point Cook (approximately 20 miles outside of Melbourne) for the flight to Perth, because it had a better runway for the take-off than what was available at Sydney. This was important because they were carrying a load of 900 gallons of petrol. This flight of 2090 miles gave both the Radio Operator and Navigator prior experience before the Tasman flight. It was mainly over land and a safe way to get acquainted with their equipment. It must be remembered that the flight was between the 8th and 9th of August 1928, and transport from any where on the Eastern side of Australia to the West, at that time or vice versa was extremely slow. Although the Trans-Australian railways had, by then been operating for a number of years, it still took days to travel from East to West and any flying would have been in stages and by charter only. This being so, Smithy and Ulm's achievement of crossing the Continent in less than a day (in a time of 23 hours and 25 minutes) was another successful flight for Smithy and Ulm, and this again gave them further publicity for their ultimate objective of operating an airline (firstly within Australia). Although Smithy said it would only be a canter after the Pacific flight, which no doubt was true, the fact that the flight took less than 24 hours was a considerable achievement for those days. As everyone knows, Australia is a large island continent covering a very large area of land and sparsely populated. As a result, time and distance between populations was and still is an important matter, something that relates back to when the First Fleet arrived in Australia and settlement started to spread out. Distance and time was always a problem, and even today politicians from Western Australia and the Northern Territory find it time consuming when they try to get back home from Canberra for a short break, so one can imagine how important that first flight across the continent, non stop was at that time, and the impression it made on people. It was obvious that the Prime Minister realised the importance of Smithy and Ulm's achievement, because distance and time taken, whether it is for people or merchandise, would always be an important factor in the development of Australia, something the Prime Minister was always very much aware of. In regards to the flight itself, it was another case of having to fly through very bad weather. So, Smithy decided to climb to 9000 feet to avoid the heavy rain but this made matters worse, because as the rain froze, Smithy and Ulm were pelted with sharp particles of ice on their face. This was another case of flying in those days, when it was impossible to climb high enough to avoid bad weather. When they eventually arrived at Perth, there was not many there to greet them which was very unusual after the Pacific flight, as wherever Smithy went there were crowds to greet him. The lack of people on his arrival at Perth, was due to a simple error as a result of giving the wrong arrival time. When giving the estimated arrival time at Perth, McWilliams gave Melbourne time which they were keeping on the flight, and this was two hours ahead of Perth. But many in Perth, saw the Southern Cross fly over the City and they soon made for Maylands Aerodrome, and it wasn't very long before a large crowd arrived. Smithy had many friends in Perth where he spent three years as a Pilot with West Australian Airways, and the hospitality given him by friends and others was such that their stay in Perth was longer than originally planned. In addition, Maylands Aerodrome was soaked after a build up of heavy rain and there was no hope of taking off until things improved. Eventually the ground dried up enough for a take off with limited fuel to get to Tammin, 90 miles east of Perth. Although the ground there was a little better than Maylands, he could only take on enough fuel to fly to Adelaide, and another large crowd was there to receive him. On this trip, McWilliams set a radio record for the Southern Cross by hearing Adelaide while they were 1350 miles away. From there, Smithy flew direct to Sydney non-stop after flying across Australia, covering a distance of 4390 miles. There was no doubt that this flight created a lot of interest in what aviation could do for distance travel in Australia. In addition it gave both Litchfield and McWilliams very valuable experience for the trip ahead to New Zealand. IMPORTANCE OF THE TRANS TRANSMAN CROSSING It was obvious that the experience of the initial flight to New Zealand was twofold. Smithy and Charles Ulm required certain information before they could safely embark on the Tasman flight, but they also wanted the Governments of both countries to get involved. Because ultimately it was an airline that they were aiming for. This airline would give faster mail delivery and quicker transportation to the people between the two countries. The correspondence before and after this flight,with the Prime Ministers of Australia and New Zealand emphasise the importance of the Tasman flight (bearing in mind that was in 1928). The careful planning and the detail of this flight was not an isolated case. All Smithy's pioneering flights while with, and greatly assisted by Charles Ulm's organising ability, and later (after Smithy and Ulm parted company), when he flew with other crews and while flying solo, had the same thoroughness in planning applied. He was not the reckless type that some have tried to portray. He was a man with a genuine desire to pioneer flights in many parts of the world, that had not previously been crossed by air. Smithy’s ultimate objective was proving what could be done in aviation, and to actively operate an airline, firstly within Australia and then an Airline across the Tasman and the Pacific. However, that was not to be his reward for all he had done. He was up against vested interest who could see his achievements as a threat to some existing airlines such as Imperial Airways, who had planned to expand further over what was then the British Empire. Shipping lines such as the Union Steamship Company ,who already had the majority of the shipping business across the Tasman Sea, could also see aviation as a threat and therefore they wanted to be prepared for at least a share in any aviation service across the Tasman. When Tasman Airways (a predecessor of Air New Zealand) was first established, the Union Steamship Company used their muscle and became a partner in Tasman Airways. In preparation for the crossing, The Prime Minister of Australia's Cablegram to the Prime Minister of New Zealand dated the 10th July 1928 lays out the detail that he received from his department of Defence. This followed a prompt reply by cablegram from the New Zealand Prime Minister dated the 13th July 1928 and the Prime Minister of Australia's reply dated the 14th July 1928. These emphasise the part and the interest the Prime Ministers of both countries had in the success of the Tasman flight. All cablegrams were confirmed by letters.
