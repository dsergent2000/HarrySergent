“Righting Wrongs” Whilst Harry was working at Mascot, Smithy and his crew survived a near tragedy over the Tasman Sea. The historical accounts of this incident, was something Harry was extremely passionate about, as he felt it was incorrectly reported upon by several prominent voices, and he vigorously set out to amend any false claims on the incident. The disputed incident occured on May I5th, 1935. Smithy, P.G. 'Bill' Taylor and John S.W. Stannage were again flying to New Zealand aboard the "Southern Cross". However, when they were 700 miles across the Tasman Sea, one of the propellers was severely damaged, and they were left flying with only one engine. In an act of both ingenuity and desperation the crew had an idea, which resulted in Bill climbing out on the wing whilst in mid-air in attempt to transfer the engine’s oil. For the transfer they had the idea of removing a flask’s inner glass, and tying a shirt around it for better grip, and transferring the oil using a leather satchel. Miraculously, Bill did climb out on the wing, the oil transfer was successfully carried out, and they arrived safely. This feat has been viewed as an amazing and courageous moment in aviation history. Unfortunately, an old flying colleague of Smithy’s named Scotty Allan has portrayed the incident and other moments of Smithy’s career as being false. Harry said from all records, Scotty Allan was a good pilot, but was 'a very vindictive old bastard'. His insults toward Smithy were potent to Harry, as Harry had the highest of respect for Smithy. Thus, Harry, Bruce Cowan and many of their colleagues were deeply upset that Allan’s version of the famous oil change and other historical events were sensationalized and full of inaccurate information. Due to these negative portrayals, both Harry and Bruce felt it needed to be a major part of their book to retell the truth of this moment, and back it up with undeniable facts. Thus, Harry collected countless documents, newspaper articles and photos from national archives of the incident. The correcting of this matter was a serious matter to Harry, and was to be a major section of his novel.
