“Those Early Days” Plan Sadly, Harry’s memoirs were never completed due to time, and molecular degeneration. He also unfortunately lost a large piece of writing due to a computer issue, which was a major set back. Below are some notes Harry wrote, as his guide he intended to include in the remainder of his memoirs. - The order of layout is to be in two parts. Firstly, the story of Kingsford Smith and my time with Smithy Barnstorming in NZ in 1934, and followed with my time at Kingsford Smith Air Service at Mascot. - Also, Smithy’s miraculous take-off at Old Bonalbo, Northern NSW after their plane crashed landed, January 1st 1930. The plane was repaired but it was realised the paddock was too short for a normal take off. The story goes that Sir Charles Kingsford Smith turned up to retrieve the plane. There was a large gum tree nearby and he apparently asked that a rope be tied to the rear of the plane and the engines throttled up to full power. The rope was cut with an axe and the plane then after a much shorter taxi than normal made it safely into the air. - Then, my time with Imperial Airways and B.O.A.C. on a world wide contract. - Memories such as Brindisi, Egypt, Kisumu, Anglo Egyptian, Sudan, etc, and stories of time spent in these places other than general aviation experiences. - My transfer to Tasman Airways which was not very long due to my transfer from Calcutta being delayed. - Working at factories there where I was allocated by what was known as the “Man Power Board” during the War. - My later attempt to join the RAF Ferry Command and my final attempt to get there via Auckland when the American Consulate who I had to report to suggested I might accept a job as an Ordinary Seaman to replace an injured seaman on a Liberty Boat, which was in Auckland at that time. I was told that it should not take too long to get to America and I could them travel to Canada, via whatever means was available. - Four months at sea travelling around the Pacific Islands during the war and finally arriving at Dorval too late to join the RAF Ferry Command. - Because of my references while working for De Havilland in Australia on the Mosquito Bomber, it was suggested that I should go to De Havilland in Toronto which I had no alternative but to do, seeing I was then in Canada. When I arrived at De Havilland and they saw my references I was employed as an Inspector on the assembly of the Mosquito Bomber, which was also built in Canada (as well as in England and Australia). After that, I returned to BOAC and finished my time in aviation with them, and I then returned to Australia.
